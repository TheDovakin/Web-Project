1. Vaquita (Phocoena sinus)
        Status: Critically Endangered

        Location: Northern part of the Gulf of California, Mexico

        Population: Fewer than 10 individuals remaining

        Threats:

        Accidental entanglement in illegal gillnets used for fishing (especially for totoaba fish)

        Habitat degradation

        Interesting Fact: The vaquita is the smallest cetacean in the world and was only discovered in 1958.

2. Hawksbill Sea Turtle (Eretmochelys imbricata)
        Status: Critically Endangered

        Location: Tropical coral reefs around the world (e.g., Caribbean, Indo-Pacific)

        Threats:

        Illegal trade of their beautiful shell (used for jewelry)

        Loss of nesting sites

        Climate change affecting nesting temperatures and reef health

        Interesting Fact: Hawksbills play a crucial role in maintaining the health of coral reefs by eating sponges.

3. Giant Manta Ray (Mobula birostris)
        Status: Endangered

        Location: Worldwide in tropical and subtropical oceans

        Threats:

        Targeted and bycatch fishing (their gill plates are used in traditional Chinese medicine)

        Pollution and microplastics

        Boat strikes

        Interesting Fact: They are among the largest rays, with a wingspan that can reach up to 7 meters (23 feet), and are incredibly intelligent.

4. Blue Whale (Balaenoptera musculus)
        Status: Endangered

        Location: All major oceans (except the Arctic)

        Threats:

        Ship strikes

        Ocean noise pollution (affects communication)

        Climate change and prey availability

        Interesting Fact: The blue whale is the largest animal on Earth, growing up to 30 meters (100 feet) long and weighing over 150 tons!

5. Scalloped Hammerhead Shark (Sphyrna lewini)
        Status: Critically Endangered

        Location: Coastal warm temperate and tropical seas worldwide

        Threats:

        Overfishing for their fins (shark fin soup trade)

        Bycatch

        Habitat loss (especially mangroves for juveniles)

        Interesting Fact: Their wide, flattened head (cephalofoil) gives them excellent 360-degree vision.
